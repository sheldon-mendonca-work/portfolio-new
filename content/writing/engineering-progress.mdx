As a Mechanical Engineering graduate with 4 years in aerospace, life was good. I was leading projects, talking directly with customers and managing team billing by my third year.
The turning point was an Excel VBA tool used for automating calculations and reports. I was given the password to debug it and hunting down crashes and unrecoverable files gave me a genuine interest in programming. I wanted to automate things properly.


At 4 YOE as a Senior Stress Engineer, I decided to switch to software. It's easier to make that jump at 4 years than at 6, so I took the leap.


I had some background in C and MATLAB, but enrolled in a full-stack course to get closer to industry standards. On the side I picked up Node.js, PostgreSQL and Docker. The best thing I learned outside the course was Data Structures and Algorithms - time and space complexity, problem solving in C. I use these regularly in front-end work and would have deep regret if I'd missed them.


I joined HireHunch, an early-stage startup, as a front-end engineer. From day one I owned my tasks. Within six months the complexity doubled and my sleep halved, but I became 10x the engineer I was when I joined. When my senior left, I took over everything - code, deployments, pipelines, AWS. The projects have only gotten more complex since.


Was switching worth it? Yes.

When I was doing structural calculations, I thought about automation constantly but couldn't act on it. Now I can. One day I hope to bring that back and make the mechanical and mechatronics fields more efficient.
